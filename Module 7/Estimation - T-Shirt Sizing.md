# Estimation: T-Shirt Sizing

> [!note] Overview
> ==T-shirt sizing== is a lightweight estimation technique that assigns relative size labels (XS, S, M, L, XL, XXL) to tasks instead of specific numbers. It provides a ==high-level estimate without exact figures==, making it fast to apply, easy to understand, and flexible enough for early-stage planning and workload management.

---

## 1. What Is T-Shirt Sizing?

T-shirt sizing estimates the effort or time required for tasks by assigning familiar size labels. Each size represents a ==relative amount of work==, not a fixed number of hours or days.

> [!example] Size Range Example
> - **S (Small)**: a few hours of effort
> - **M (Medium)**: roughly a day or two
> - **L (Large)**: several days
> - **XL (Extra Large)**: a week or more

The exact mapping depends on the team's agreement — there is no universal conversion.

---

## 2. Factors That Influence T-Shirt Sizes

T-shirt sizing can reflect a combination of dimensions:

| Factor | What It Captures |
|---|---|
| ==Scope== | How many components, systems, or areas are involved |
| ==Effort== | Total labor required across all roles |
| ==Complexity== | Technical or functional difficulty |
| ==Work hours== | Approximate time to complete |
| ==Time estimate== | Calendar duration including overhead |

Teams should agree upfront on ==which factors their sizes represent== — effort only, or a blend of effort and complexity — to keep estimates consistent.

---

## 3. Six Guidelines for Effective T-Shirt Sizing

### Guideline 1: Define the Sizes

> [!info] Define Sizes Before You Start
> Agree on which size labels to use before beginning estimation. ==Limit the number of sizes== to prevent confusion.

Common options:
- **4 sizes**: S, M, L, XL — suitable for most backlogs
- **5 sizes**: XS, S, M, L, XL — adds a bucket for trivial tasks
- **6 sizes**: XS, S, M, L, XL, XXL — for very wide-ranging project scopes

---

### Guideline 2: Clarify What Each Size Means

> [!info] Shared Understanding Is Essential
> Every team member must understand what each size signifies. Provide ==concrete examples== from your team's own context to illustrate the differences.

Define whether sizes represent:
- Effort alone
- Complexity alone
- A combination of effort, complexity, and time

> [!example] Anchoring Sizes to Real Work
> "A Medium is roughly like the login page redesign we did last sprint. A Large is closer to the payment integration from Q1."

---

### Guideline 3: Assign Responsibility

> [!info] Who Assigns the Sizes?
> Decide in advance who is responsible for assigning T-shirt sizes:

| Role | When Appropriate |
|---|---|
| Product Owner | Sizing product backlog items by business value/scope |
| Scrum Master | Facilitating Agile team estimation sessions |
| Individual team members | Sizing based on collective technical understanding |

In practice, ==collaborative estimation== (where the whole team weighs in) produces the most accurate and trusted results.

---

### Guideline 4: Assign Sizes to All Existing Work

> [!info] Retroactive and Ongoing Sizing
> Retroactively assign T-shirt sizes to existing backlog items, and ensure every ==new task is sized before work begins==. This creates a shared understanding across the team about the relative effort of upcoming work.

---

### Guideline 5: Track Sizes in a Work Management Tool

> [!info] Use Tooling for Visibility
> Track T-shirt sizes in a shared work management platform (e.g., Jira, Asana, Linear). This provides:
> - Visibility into individual and team workloads
> - Transparency for stakeholders and managers
> - A foundation for capacity planning and sprint composition

---

### Guideline 6: Use Sizes for Workload Management

> [!info] Workload Balancing
> T-shirt sizes are not just for planning — they are also a ==workload management tool==. By tracking the total size of work assigned to each team member, you can:
> - Prevent burnout from overloading individuals
> - Ensure equitable distribution of effort
> - Spot bottlenecks before they become crises

---

## 4. T-Shirt Sizing vs. Story Points

| Aspect | T-Shirt Sizing | Story Points |
|---|---|---|
| Format | Labels (S, M, L, XL) | Numbers (1, 2, 3, 5, 8…) |
| Speed | Very fast, low friction | Slightly more deliberate |
| Precision | Low — intentionally rough | Medium — relative but more granular |
| Best for | Early planning, initiative sizing, roadmaps | Sprint-level backlog refinement |
| Velocity tracking | Harder — labels don't aggregate easily | Easy — points sum to a velocity |
| Stakeholder communication | Intuitive, non-technical audiences | Better for engineering teams |

---

## 5. When to Use T-Shirt Sizing

> [!example] Ideal Use Cases
> - **Early project planning**: when requirements are still rough and exact sizing would be premature
> - **Roadmap-level estimation**: sizing epics or initiatives rather than individual user stories
> - **Cross-team alignment**: communicating effort to stakeholders unfamiliar with Fibonacci or story points
> - **Quick backlog triage**: rapidly sorting items by size to prioritize or de-scope
> - **Workload distribution**: balancing work across team members without over-engineering the process

---

## Summary

T-shirt sizing is a ==fast, flexible, and intuitive== estimation method that trades numerical precision for speed and clarity. Its six guidelines — defining sizes, clarifying their meaning, assigning responsibility, retroactively sizing existing work, tracking in tooling, and using sizes for workload management — make it a structured yet lightweight practice. It is most powerful at the ==initiative and roadmap level==, where precision is premature but relative sizing still adds significant planning value.
