# Estimation: Purpose

> [!note] Overview
> ==Estimation== serves far more than scheduling. Project leaders rely on it continuously — during planning and throughout execution — to make smart decisions, assess feasibility, set goals, and stay in control. As Tom DeMarco famously stated: *"You can't manage what you can't control, and you can't control what you don't measure."*

## Why Estimation Is Always Needed

Project leaders need estimates at every stage: when deciding whether to take on work, when setting objectives, and when tracking progress. Steve McConnell argues that a ==good estimate gives a clear picture of the project== and helps leaders make informed decisions to keep it on track.

The goal of estimation is to give a ==clear, realistic prediction== of how long or how much a project will take. This benefits both the customer and the technical team by enabling better decisions, easier adjustment of goals and processes, and a higher likelihood of delivering quality results.

---

## The Four Primary Purposes of Estimation

### 1. Provide Realistic Information for Decision-Making

> [!info] Purpose 1
> Offer ==accurate, realistic insights== to guide decision-making throughout the entire project lifecycle.

This is the most foundational purpose. Estimation provides:
- Accurate insights into **what can be achieved** and **by when**
- Management of expectations when top management sets ==unrealistic goals==
- A basis for corrective actions when expectations diverge from technical reality

> [!warning] Common Scenario
> When top management sets an unrealistic deadline, the estimation reveals the gap. This prompts adjustments such as:
> - Scope reduction
> - Team augmentation
> - Third-party collaboration

---

### 2. Decide Whether to Participate in a Project

> [!info] Purpose 2
> Use estimations as a guide to make ==informed decisions about project participation==.

Solution architects often work alongside sales teams, who are incentivized to close deals — sometimes by overpromising to clients. However, the technical team bears the responsibility of delivery.

| Consideration | Question to Answer |
|---|---|
| Expertise fit | Does this project align with our skills? |
| Profitability | Will this project be financially viable? |
| Risk exposure | Could this lead to financial losses or legal issues? |
| Strategic value | Is partnering with this client worth the risk? |

> [!example] Real-World Scenario
> A high-profile client (e.g., Google or BMW) presents an opportunity. The project seems attractive, but estimation reveals it falls outside the team's expertise or is financially unviable. The right decision may be to decline — avoiding losses and reputational damage.

---

### 3. Set the Project's Objectives and Goals

> [!info] Purpose 3
> Define ==clear, prioritized objectives== that give the team a shared vision and direction.

This involves:
- Understanding the project's **purpose** and **overarching goals**
- ==Prioritizing goals== to focus effort on the most critical outcomes
- Aligning stakeholders' expectations around a unified vision
- Creating a roadmap for project execution

> [!tip] Why Prioritization Matters
> Not all goals are equal. Estimation helps identify which outcomes deliver the most value and which are "nice to have," ensuring effort is concentrated where it matters most.

---

### 4. Plan and Control the Project to Meet Targets

> [!info] Purpose 4
> Use the estimation roadmap for ==ongoing monitoring, control, and proactive adjustment==.

With estimates in hand, the team can:
- Compare **actual progress** against the estimated roadmap
- Detect deviations early and respond proactively
- Make iterative adjustments to stay aligned with project objectives

> [!example] Iterative Control Loop
> 1. Estimate → create roadmap
> 2. Execute → track actuals
> 3. Compare actuals to roadmap
> 4. Adjust if deviations are found
> 5. Repeat throughout the project lifecycle

---

## Comparison at a Glance

| Purpose | What It Answers | Who Benefits |
|---|---|---|
| Realistic decision-making info | What can we achieve, and by when? | All stakeholders |
| Project participation decision | Should we take on this work? | Management, architects |
| Set objectives and goals | What are we trying to achieve? | Team, stakeholders |
| Plan and control | Are we on track? | Project managers, team |

---

## Summary

Estimation is not a one-time activity — it is an ongoing practice that serves four critical purposes: providing realistic information for decisions, evaluating whether to accept a project, setting clear goals, and controlling execution. Together these purposes make estimation ==a core responsibility of the technical team== and a key enabler of project success.
