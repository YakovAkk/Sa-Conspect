# Estimation: Definitions

> [!note] Overview
> ==Estimation== in software engineering is not limited to time. It is a technical forecast that covers ==costs, effort, size, value, and risk== — prepared by the technical team to support decision-making. Understanding what an estimate truly is — and how it differs from a target or a commitment — is foundational to project planning.

## 1. What Is an Estimate?

An ==estimate== is a prediction about the future outcome of a project. It is a technical forecast usually prepared by the technical team, covering one or more of the following dimensions: time, cost, effort, size, value, and risk.

### Expert Definitions

> [!info] Steve McConnell
> "An estimate is a **prediction of how long a project will take or how much it will cost**."

> [!info] Capers Jones
> "Predicting the future outcome of a project in terms of various factors, including **sizes, schedules, effort, costs, value, and risk**."

> [!info] Tom DeMarco
> "An estimate is a **prediction that is equally likely to be above or below the actual result**."

### Key Characteristics

| Characteristic | Description |
|---|---|
| Prepared by | Technical team |
| Purpose | Inform decision-making |
| Nature | Probabilistic — can be above or below actuality |
| Scope | Time, cost, effort, size, value, risk |

---

## 2. Estimate vs. Target vs. Commitment

These three terms are often confused but serve very different purposes in a project.

### Estimate
A ==technical forecast== prepared by the technical team. It predicts costs, effort, and risk to help with decision-making. It is objective and probability-based.

### Target
A ==business-set goal== reflecting what the business wants to achieve. It is based on strategic goals, market demands, or contractual obligations — not derived from technical analysis.

### Commitment
A ==shared promise== to achieve agreed-upon results, made jointly by both the technical and business teams. It goes beyond goal-setting: it implies mutual accountability and responsibility for the project's success.

---

## 3. Comparison at a Glance

| Aspect | Estimate | Target | Commitment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Who sets it | Technical team | Business / stakeholders | Both teams jointly |
| Based on | Technical analysis | Strategic goals | Agreed expectations |
| Nature | Predictive, probabilistic | Aspirational | Binding, shared |
| Purpose | Decision support | Business alignment | Accountability |
| Can be wrong? | Yes — it's a forecast | Yes — may be unrealistic | Should be honored |

---

## 4. Why Estimation Matters

> [!tip] The Role of the Technical Team
> Technical team members play a critical role in producing estimates. Their involvement demonstrates their value to the project: they are the ones who best understand effort, complexity, and risk.

- Estimates enable ==informed decision-making== about scope, schedule, and budget.
- Without reliable estimates, targets become guesses and commitments become unreliable.
- Estimation spans multiple dimensions: not just "how long?" but also "how much?", "how risky?", and "how valuable?".

---

## 5. When to Choose Which

> [!example] Decision Hints
> - Use an **estimate** when you need an objective, data-driven prediction for planning or negotiation.
> - Use a **target** to communicate what business success looks like (but don't confuse it with an estimate).
> - Use a **commitment** when both teams have agreed on a shared outcome and accept mutual accountability.
> - Never substitute a **target** for an **estimate** — doing so ignores technical reality and sets the project up for failure.

---

## Summary

Estimation is a multi-dimensional technical practice, not just "how many days will this take." Experts like McConnell, Jones, and DeMarco define it as a probabilistic prediction covering size, schedule, effort, cost, value, and risk. Distinguishing an estimate (technical forecast) from a target (business goal) and a commitment (shared promise) is essential to healthy project communication and realistic planning.
