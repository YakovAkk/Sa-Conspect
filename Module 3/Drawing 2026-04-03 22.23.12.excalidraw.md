Explicitly and Implicitly

Implicit ASRs are not always explicitly stated but can be inferred from context.
For example, high-risk requirements, high-priority, or those with low stability are often considered architecturally significant.

Explicit ASRs are clearly written and often describe specific technical specifications.
Examples include the product must support multiple languages (localization), data will be stored in a non-relational database, 
such as MongoDB, 
the system must run 24/7, and an online help system must be included.

Understanding Architecturally Significant Requirements
 ^MMPdXgDp

You need to recognize ASRs early before starting the architecture design and implementation. 
If important ASRs are missed at the beginning, it can cause project delays and budget overruns. 
When an overlooked ASR is discovered later, the customer may request changes, which may require updates to the target architecture. 
Making such changes during implementation usually adds extra time and cost to the project. ^ftfNgJFx

Architecturally Significant Requirements Key Factors
 ^Fhy0LY6z

Stakeholder Benefits
Understand how a requirement benefits stakeholders. Requirements that are crucial, important, or highly valuable to stakeholders are often architecturally significant.
For example, a feature that directly improves user experience or customer satisfaction may have a strong architectural impact.
Architectural Impact
Evaluating how a requirement affects the system's architecture is fundamental. Some requirements may cause only small changes, 
while others can reshape or extend the entire architecture. Even a small requirement can have a major impact if it affects core components or their interactions.
Risks Mitigation
Consider the risks linked to each requirement. Requirements that improve performance, availability, or security, 
or that confirm the suitability of certain components, are often architecturally significant. These requirements help reduce major risks to the system's stability and reliability.
Coverage Completion
Confirm that the architecture fully addresses all necessary aspects. Architecturally significant requirements help close critical gaps,
ensuring the architecture is complete and can handle different scenarios effectively.

Gathering ASRs by Interviewing Stakeholders
 ^bf7Odqa7

Step 1: Maintain a List of potential ASRs
Maintain a comprehensive list of potential ASRs for discussion, even if not all apply 
to the specific project.
Step 2: Formulate Questions
Develop clear and understandable questions for each architectural requirement, 
ensuring stakeholders can comprehend them.
Step 3: Explain the Format
Clearly explain the interview format to stakeholders, emphasizing the discussion's importance 
and impact on the future solution.
Step 4: Capture the Responses
Document stakeholder responses to the questions, incorporating their priorities or
weightings for each architectural requirement. 
This prioritization aids architects in making necessary tradeoffs.
Step 5: Prioritize ASRs
Through several iterations, refine and prioritize the architectural requirements, resulting 
in a final list of Architecturally Significant Requirements. ^Y9gbxxgW

Characteristics of Quality Attribute Workshop
 ^cEGl0qqs

The Quality Attribute Workshop is a structured approach to engaging system stakeholders early in the life cycle 
to identify essential quality attribute requirements for a software-intensive system.
There are four key characteristics of the QAW:
System-Centric
The QAW discusses the future solution, organized by a solution architect with all stakeholders participating.
Use Case Consensus
The QAW aims to establish consensus among stakeholders regarding use cases.
Timely coordination
The QAW is scheduled within the project timeline, preceding the software architecture design process.
Scenario-based
Solution architects present scenarios for key use cases, facilitating discussions and defining quality attributes and metrics. ^I8gKt8CD

SEI Quality Attribute Workshop Phases
Quality Attribute Workshops (QAWs) provide a method for identifying a system's architecture-critical quality attributes, such as availability, performance, security, interoperability, and modifiability, that are derived from mission or business goals. The QAW does not assume the existence of a software architecture.
QAWs have some phases

Prepare
Duration: varies
Meeting: e-mail, call
A solution achitect: 
 - Prepares scenarios and other documents for discussion
 - Reaches an agreement about when the QAW will be conducted
 - Helps to select relevant stakeholders on customer side
 - Develops agenda for the QAW

Conduct
Duration: 1-2 days
Meeting: typically at customer side
A solution architect:
 - Facilitates the workshop
 - Drives the workshop in order to get the right outcome

Report
Duration: varies
Meeting: e-mail, call
A solution architect:
 - Processes stakeholders’ feedback
 - Builds a joint picture based on the information from the workshop’s participants
 - Describes quality attributes, as well as metrics to measure them ^J8NzyjUw

Tracing ASRs to Quality Attributes and Common Pitfalls
 ^ExIb276E

Utility Trees
Step 1 The foundation of a Utility Tree begins with the term "utility," representing the overall desirability of the system.
Step 2 Elaborate on the root node by listing the primary quality attributes the system must demonstrate.
Step 3 For each quality attribute, detail specific refinements like "data latency" or "transaction throughput."
Step 4 Customize the refinements based on the system's relevance. Align each refinement with the appropriate ASRs, 
often expressed as QA scenarios. Ensure each ASR focuses on specific concerns, avoiding overly diverse territories.
Step 5 Evaluate the recorded ASRs against two criteria: their business value and architectural impact. 
Employ a simple scale such as "H" (high), "M" (medium), and "L" (low) for each criterion.
 ^egLCLtDN

Example: ^co4huuQF

Prioritizing Architecturally Significant Requirements
For Business Value
High: Indispensable requirements critical for the project's success.
Medium: Important features that, if omitted, won't lead to project failure.
Low: Desirable functionalities with minimal impact, worth considering but not crucial.

For Architectural Impact
High: Requirements significantly shaping the system's architecture.
Medium: Features with noticeable architectural influence.
Low: Minimal effect on the overall system architecture. ^ukQ9yLVF

Common Pitfalls of Gathering ASRs
 ^WAYlxAKO

"The "Shopping Cart" Mentality - when person thinks that we can add lots of features without consequences and does not think about it
The "This Is Too Technical for Me" Attitude - when a stakeholder thinks that questionary that Solution Achitect has given them is too "technical"
and they can skip it, SA should encorage them and provide examples why it is nessesary to answer
The "All Requirements Are Equal" Fallacy - a common misconception that all requirements aer equal, SA should strongly prioritase them besause them can be ASR,
also it maks impossimbe to make a proper system design without prioritasing
The "Requirements That Can't Be Measured" Syndrome - Both domain-specific and architectural requirements must be precise and measurable. 
The "Not Enough Time" Complaint - Specifying all the ASR is very vital task for system's foundation, if stakeholders express impatience remind them how 
significantly important is establishing a strong foundation for the system.
The "All Stakeholders Are Alike" Misconception - Different stakeholders contribute diverse perspectives during the requirements-gathering process. 
Recognizing these varied inputs is crucial. Identify specific stakeholder groups responsible for answering relevant questions. 
For instance, product management may provide insights on licensing elements, while end users are best suited to define usability requirements. 
Tailoring questions to the appropriate stakeholders ensures that the gathered requirements align with the unique needs and expectations of each group."
