# Fixed-Price Pricing Model

> [!note] Overview
> The ==fixed-price== model is a contractual arrangement where the **total project cost is predetermined and agreed upon before work begins**. It guarantees a fixed budget regardless of time and expenses incurred. Most suitable for projects with **clearly defined scopes** and minimal expected changes.

## 1. When Fixed-Price Fits

> [!info] Ideal Scenarios
> - Projects with **clearly defined scopes** and stable requirements
> - Clients with **strict budget requirements**
> - Funding from specific sources like **grants**
> - **Short durations** (typically 1–3 months)
> - **Predictable timelines**

## 2. Advantages

> [!info] Strengths
> - **Clarity** — well-defined requirements and milestones
> - **Transparency** — promotes effective planning
> - **Financial security** — budget and timeline guaranteed
> - **Minimal client supervision** — vendor manages all aspects
> - **Good fit for small trial projects**

## 3. Disadvantages

> [!warning] Weaknesses
> - **Scope changes are difficult** — require negotiations and additional costs
> - **Quality risk** — if costs are underestimated, vendor may cut corners
> - **Lengthy initiation** — detailed documentation and frequent meetings required
> - **Not ideal for evolving needs** — bad fit for frequent changes or major business-process impact

## Advantages vs. Disadvantages

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|
| Clear requirements and milestones | Hard to adjust scope |
| Budget and timeline guaranteed | Cost-cutting risk if underestimated |
| Minimal client supervision | Long project initiation |
| Suitable for small trials | Bad fit for evolving requirements |

## 4. How the Process Works

> [!example] Typical Flow
> 1. Define **clear scope and requirements** in detail.
> 2. Estimate **total cost and timeline** comprehensively.
> 3. **Sign contract** with fixed price.
> 4. Vendor manages all execution within the agreed budget.
> 5. Any scope change requires a **change order**.

## 5. Why Organizations Choose It

> [!tip] Strategic Reasons
> - **Predictability** is more valuable than flexibility.
> - **Stakeholder approval** is easier with a known total.
> - **Vendor accountability** for delivering within budget.
> - **Reduced internal management overhead**.

## Common Pitfalls

> [!warning] What Breaks Fixed-Price Deals
> - **Undefined exclusions** — scope creep through "everyone assumed it was included".
> - **Missing change management** — every change becomes a fight.
> - **Underestimated complexity** — vendor cuts quality to stay profitable.
> - **No buffer** — first risk that fires sinks the project margin.

## Summary

The fixed-price model offers **budget predictability, financial security, and clear milestones** in exchange for **scope rigidity**. It works well for **short, well-defined projects** with stable requirements but fails badly for evolving or high-uncertainty work. Detailed upfront documentation and explicit change-order processes are essential to keep the model fair to both sides.
