# Working on Questions: Grouping, Responsibility and Examples

> [!note] Overview
> At the beginning of the RFP question phase, ==group questions into topics== — typically **scope, domain, technical, and timeline** — and assign team members to each group based on **expertise and roles**. Questions are exchanged with the client; when answers are unavailable, decisions rely on assumptions, risks, or escalation.

## 1. Group Questions by Topic

> [!info] Typical Categories
> - **Scope**
> - **Domain**
> - **Technical**
> - **Timeline**

The exact categories are flexible — split questions to suit your team's workflow and expertise.

## 2. Assign Responsibility

### Scope and Technical Questions
==Business Analysts== are typically responsible — they bring an understanding of **business requirements and system functionalities**.

### Domain Questions
==Solution Architects== provide insights and guidance — drawing from their expertise in **relevant technologies and industry practices**.

### Timeline Clarification
==Delivery Managers / Project Managers== address questions about **project schedules and milestones**.

## Responsibility Summary

| Question Type | Owner | Expertise Brought |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | Business Analyst | Business requirements |
| Technical | Business Analyst (with architect input) | System functionalities |
| Domain | Solution Architect | Tech + industry practices |
| Timeline | Delivery / Project Manager | Schedule, milestones |

## 3. The Question Exchange

> [!tip] Bidirectional Flow
> Questions are shared between **vendor and client** to obtain necessary information. Sometimes there are no answers — in those cases, decisions are made using:
> - **Assumptions**
> - **Risks**
> - **Escalation** (involving more people)

## 4. Typical Customer Responses

> [!info] What You'll Hear From Clients
> - "Yes"
> - "Most likely no"
> - "Subject to discovery"
> - References back to the RFP documents

> [!warning] Repeated "Check the RFP" Is a Signal
> If the client repeatedly tells you to check the RFP documents, that often means the **RFP itself needs a careful review and possible changes** — not that your question was wrong.

## 5. Example Questions

> [!example] Sample RFP Questions
> - Do we have a vendor / API for **SMS notification**, or should it be part of the solution?
> - Are there any **constraints or client preferences** — tools, technologies, servers, processes?
> - Could you provide examples of **custom rules** that the User manages?
> - What is the allowed **monthly outage time** for each application?

## Why Categorization Matters

> [!tip] Strategic Reasons
> - **Parallelism** — different specialists can work in parallel on their groups.
> - **Quality** — each question goes to the person best equipped to answer it.
> - **No gaps** — categorization makes missing topics visible.
> - **Audit trail** — clear ownership simplifies follow-ups.

## Summary

Working on RFP questions begins by **grouping them by topic** (scope, domain, technical, timeline) and **assigning them by expertise** (BA, Solution Architect, Delivery Manager). Questions flow in **both directions** between client and vendor. When answers are missing, the team falls back to assumptions, risks, or escalation. Repeated client deflection to the RFP documents is a meaningful signal that the RFP itself may need review.
