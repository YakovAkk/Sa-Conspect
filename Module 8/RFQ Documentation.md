# RFQ Documentation: Formats and Pricing Details

> [!note] Overview
> Companies typically respond to RFQs using ==Excel files== or ==tender management tools==. These formats are flexible — they make it easy to adjust prices across multiple scenarios, so each offer can be tailored to the client's specific needs and budget.

## 1. Common Formats

> [!info] Typical RFQ Response Formats
> - **Excel files** — easy to edit, calculate, and compare line-by-line
> - **Tender management tools** — structured, often required by enterprise procurement
>
> Both formats support quick adjustments for different scenarios (volume, scope variants, optional services).

> [!tip] Why Flexibility Matters
> Pricing rarely fits a single scenario. Clients ask for variants — different durations, optional modules, support tiers. A flexible format lets vendors **adjust quickly without rebuilding the document**.

## 2. What RFQ Documents Include

RFQ pricing documents typically cover three categories of detail:

### 2.1 Application Costs by Year
> [!info] Year-by-Year Pricing
> - Cost for the **first year**
> - Cost for the **second year**
> - Cost for the **third year**
>
> Multi-year breakdowns let the client see total cost of ownership over the contract period.

### 2.2 Number of Tasks or Deliverables
The pricing should clearly state how many ==tasks or deliverables== are covered. This protects both sides:
- The vendor knows what is in scope.
- The client knows what they are paying for.

### 2.3 Estimated Ongoing Annual Costs
> [!info] Recurring Costs
> - **Support** services
> - **Maintenance** services
> - **Operational** services
>
> These are typically projected per year, separately from one-time application costs.

## 3. Example Structure

> [!example] Excel Layout
> An Excel file might show:
> - Yearly **support and maintenance costs** for each project year
> - Separate line items for **hosting**
> - Separate line items for other operational services
>
> This breakdown makes the proposal transparent and easy to compare against competitors.

## Cost Category Summary

| Cost Category | What It Covers | Typical Time Frame |
|---|---|---|
| Application Costs | Build / licensing / configuration | Year 1, 2, 3 |
| Task/Deliverable Pricing | Scope volume | Per project / per item |
| Support & Maintenance | Ongoing support contract | Annual recurring |
| Hosting & Operations | Infrastructure and operational services | Annual recurring |

## Best Practices for RFQ Documentation

> [!tip] What Makes a Strong RFQ Response
> - **Break costs into clear categories** — don't bundle everything into one number.
> - Show **year-by-year** figures, not just totals.
> - Separate **one-time** vs **recurring** costs.
> - Make **scope (tasks, deliverables) explicit** alongside the price.
> - Use a format that lets the client **easily adjust assumptions** and re-compare.

## Why Documentation Format Matters

> [!example] Strategic Reasons
> - **Comparability** — clients evaluate multiple bids; structured docs win attention.
> - **Negotiability** — flexible formats support quick revisions during negotiation.
> - **Transparency** — itemized costs reduce client suspicion of hidden fees.
> - **Reusability** — well-built templates become reusable across future RFQs.

## Summary

RFQ pricing is most commonly delivered via **Excel files** or **tender management tools** because both are flexible enough to adapt to client-specific scenarios. The documents typically include **application costs per year**, **scope counts (tasks/deliverables)**, and **ongoing annual costs** for support, maintenance, and operations — often with separate line items for hosting. Clear, itemized, year-by-year breakdowns are what turn an RFQ response from a single number into a transparent, comparable, negotiable offer.
