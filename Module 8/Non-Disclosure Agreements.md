# Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDA)

> [!note] Overview
> A ==Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)== is a **legal contract** between two or more parties that outlines **confidential information** they wish to share for specific purposes while **restricting access to third parties**. In software development, NDAs are commonly used when one party shares **proprietary business information** with consultants, contractors, investors, or potential licensees.

## 1. Why Sign an NDA

There are four main reasons:

### 1.1 Confidence
The NDA assures **protection of any confidential information** shared between the parties.

### 1.2 Clear Expectations
Offers insight into both parties' **legal expectations and commitments**, fostering **transparency and trust**.

### 1.3 Security
By securing **trade secrets and documents**, the NDA safeguards **sensitive information** throughout the relationship.

### 1.4 Precise Timeframe
Establishes a **timeframe during which confidentiality applies**, ensuring **clarity and accountability** for both parties.

## NDA Benefits at a Glance

| Reason | What It Provides |
|---|---|
| Confidence | Assurance of protection |
| Clear Expectations | Legal transparency and trust |
| Security | Safeguards trade secrets |
| Precise Timeframe | Duration of obligations |

## 2. When NDAs Are Not Needed

> [!warning] Skip When Information Is Generic
> An NDA may be **unnecessary** if a project involves **generic tasks** — for example:
> - Developing a **standard mobile app**
> - Building a **WordPress-based website**
>
> If no **confidential information** is disclosed, the NDA adds friction without benefit.

## 3. Typical Use Cases

> [!info] Common Scenarios
> - Sharing proprietary algorithms or business logic
> - Discussing **product roadmaps** with consultants
> - Engaging contractors with access to **customer data**
> - **Investor pitches** with sensitive financials
> - Negotiating with **potential licensees**

## 4. What an NDA Covers

> [!info] Common NDA Contents
> - Definition of **confidential information**
> - **Permitted purposes** for using the information
> - **Restrictions** on disclosure to third parties
> - **Duration** of confidentiality obligations
> - **Consequences** of breach
> - **Return or destruction** of materials at end of engagement

## 5. NDA in the RFP Context

> [!tip] NDA During RFP
> During an RFP, an NDA is typically signed **before any deep discussions** — usually at the **Intent to Bid / NDA** step of the RFP process — so both parties can share scope details freely.

## Common Pitfalls

> [!warning] What Weakens NDAs
> - **Overly broad scope** — covers things that shouldn't need protection.
> - **Indefinite duration** — bound forever to outdated information.
> - **Vague definitions** — disputes over what was actually confidential.
> - **No mutual obligation** — only one party is bound, which creates imbalance.

## Summary

A Non-Disclosure Agreement is a **legal contract** protecting confidential information shared between parties. The four reasons to sign one are **confidence, clear expectations, security, and a precise timeframe**. NDAs are not always required — generic, low-sensitivity work may not need one — but for any engagement involving **proprietary business information**, signing an NDA establishes the legal foundation for trust.
