# Presales Overall Timeline: RFI, RFQ, and RFP Requests

> [!note] Overview
> The outsourced software development lifecycle begins with the **pre-sales phase**, followed by **sales** and **production**. ==Solution Architects== are heavily involved in pre-sales and production but play a smaller role in sales. During pre-sales, the client issues formal requests to gather information and proposals from vendors. These requests come in three main forms: **RFI**, **RFQ**, and **RFP**.

## 1. Request for Information (RFI)

An RFI is used to ==gather additional information== from potential vendors.

> [!info] Key Traits
> - Goal: understand vendor **capabilities and offerings**
> - Typically issued **before** RFP and RFQ processes
> - Low formality compared to RFP
> - Used as a screening step

> [!tip] Architect's Role
> Focus on answering **technical questions** about company capabilities, technologies, and experience.

## 2. Request for Quotation (RFQ)

An RFQ is used when ==product or service specifications are already known== and discussion with bidders is unnecessary.

> [!info] Key Traits
> - **Price is the primary** selection factor
> - Specifications are fixed; no negotiation needed
> - May precede a full RFP to **gauge price ranges**
> - Faster and narrower than an RFP

## 3. Request for Proposal (RFP)

An RFP is the ==most formal pre-sales process==. The client requests solutions to address a particular problem.

> [!info] Key Traits
> - **Strict procurement rules** for content, timelines, and responses
> - Vendors propose technical solutions, not just prices
> - Highest effort for both client and vendor
> - Decisive document for selecting the winning vendor

> [!tip] Architect's Role
> Provide detailed **technical solutions**, **work breakdowns**, and **cost estimates**.

## Request Types at a Glance

| Aspect | RFI | RFQ | RFP |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary purpose | Gather info on capabilities | Get pricing for known scope | Get full solution proposal |
| Formality | Low | Medium | High |
| Selection criterion | Vendor capability fit | Price | Solution quality + price |
| Architect's focus | Technical questions | Minimal | Solution, WBS, estimates |
| Typical timing | Earliest | Before/instead of RFP | Main proposal stage |

## When to Choose Which

> [!example] Decision Hints
> - Use **RFI** when the client doesn't yet know which vendors are even capable.
> - Use **RFQ** when scope is locked and only price differs across vendors.
> - Use **RFP** when the client needs vendors to **design and propose** a solution.

## Phase-by-Phase Architect Involvement

> [!info] Where Solution Architects Engage
> - **Pre-sales** — answer RFIs, build full RFP responses (technical solutions, work breakdowns, cost estimates).
> - **Sales** — minimal involvement; contracts and SOWs are finalized.
> - **Production / Discovery** — design the system, create **proofs of concept**, review architecture.
> - **Production / Construction** — ensure implementation follows architectural rules and standards.

## Lifecycle Flow

```
Pre-sales (RFI → RFQ/RFP → Proposal)
     │
     ▼
Sales (contracts, SOW)
     │
     ▼
Production (Discovery → Construction → Transition)
```

## Summary

The pre-sales phase relies on three formal request types — **RFI** (information), **RFQ** (pricing for known scope), and **RFP** (full solution proposals). Solution Architects engage heavily in RFIs (technical answers) and RFPs (solutions, work breakdowns, estimates), step back during sales, and re-engage strongly in the production phase across **discovery and construction**. Understanding the differences between RFI, RFQ, and RFP is essential for tailoring the right level of effort to each client request.
