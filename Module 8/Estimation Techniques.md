# Estimation Techniques in Pre-Sales

> [!note] Overview
> Estimation is an essential part of the **pre-sales phase** — it predicts the **duration and cost** of a project. It depends significantly on the ==Work Breakdown Structure== and can be accomplished using several techniques, each with its own strengths.

## 1. Analogy-Based Estimation

Uses past project data to estimate the current one.

> [!info] Key Traits
> - Compares similar **completed projects**
> - Adapts and refines past efforts for the new project
> - Leverages **internal company knowledge** or publicly available data
> - Fast and simple — but degrades if projects differ significantly

## 2. Bottom-Up Estimation Based on WBS

Assesses project components at a **granular level** and sums detailed estimates.

> [!info] Key Traits
> - **Most accurate** of the techniques
> - Evaluates cost, duration, or resource needs at the **lowest WBS level**
> - Requires a complete WBS to be reliable
> - Time-intensive to perform

## 3. Parametric Models

Uses **past events and trend analysis** to forecast future projects.

> [!info] Key Traits
> - Structured estimating processes
> - **Statistical modeling** methods
> - Often based on mathematical equations
> - Integrates data from reference books, consultants, or vendors
> - Provides a basis for confident conclusions

## 4. Expert Estimation

Taps into the **insights of cost estimation experts** or groups.

> [!info] Key Traits
> - **Minimal process and structure**
> - Leverages expert experience
> - Factors in various project complexities
> - **Checklists** may help systematize the process

## 5. Three-Point Estimation

Estimates each activity with **three figures**: best-case, most likely, and worst-case scenarios.

> [!info] How It Works
> A weighted average is calculated to derive the final estimate, considering optimistic, realistic, and pessimistic outcomes.
>
> Common formula (PERT): **Expected = (Best + 4 × MostLikely + Worst) / 6**

> [!tip] Why It Helps
> Offers a comprehensive view of **project uncertainty** rather than a single number.

## Techniques at a Glance

| Technique | Strength | Best When |
|---|---|---|
| Analogy-Based | Fast, simple | Similar past projects exist |
| Bottom-Up (WBS) | Most accurate | Complete WBS available |
| Parametric Models | Data-driven, repeatable | Historical trend data exists |
| Expert Estimation | Captures judgment | Experts available, limited data |
| Three-Point | Quantifies uncertainty | Wide range of possible outcomes |

## Decision Hints

> [!example] When to Use Which
> - **Have a complete WBS?** → Bottom-Up.
> - **Have past projects?** → Analogy-Based.
> - **Have rich historical data?** → Parametric.
> - **Have experts, limited data?** → Expert Estimation.
> - **High uncertainty?** → Three-Point (and combine with the above).

## Why Use Multiple Techniques

> [!tip] Triangulation
> Combining techniques **cross-validates** results. If your bottom-up and analogy estimates align, confidence rises. If they diverge significantly, investigate before committing.

## Summary

Five common estimation techniques support pre-sales: **Analogy-Based, Bottom-Up (WBS-driven), Parametric Models, Expert Estimation, and Three-Point Estimation**. Each suits different situations. Strong pre-sales teams **combine techniques** — using bottom-up for accuracy, analogy for sanity checking, and three-point for uncertainty modeling.
