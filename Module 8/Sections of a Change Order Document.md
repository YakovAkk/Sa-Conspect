# Sections of a Change Order Document

> [!note] Overview
> Change management in software development often occurs **early in the project lifecycle**, triggered by **bug identification**, **customer feedback**, or **market requirements**. A change order document provides a **structured framework** for documenting and formalizing requested alternations — ensuring **clarity, accountability, and alignment** among stakeholders.

## 1. Change Description

> [!info] What to Include
> - **Initiator** of the change
> - **Category** (e.g., scope, design, technical, business)
> - **Scope** of the change
> - **Estimated resources** required

This section captures the **what and who** of the change.

## 2. Rationale

> [!info] What to Include
> - The **problem** being addressed
> - How the change **addresses it**
> - **Consequences** of not implementing the change
> - **Alternative solutions** considered

This section captures the **why** — and crucially, the cost of inaction.

## 3. Priority

Assessment of the change's **urgency and impact** on software development.

> [!info] Priority Levels
> - **Low** — nice to have, no urgency
> - **Medium** — should be done in the next cycle
> - **High** — needed soon to avoid downstream issues
> - **Critical** — blocking, must be addressed immediately

## 4. Impact

> [!info] What to Include
> - How the change affects **existing work processes**
> - **New tasks** created
> - **Potential risks**

This section helps decision-makers see **knock-on effects** before approving.

## 5. Course of Action

> [!info] What to Include
> - Updates to **project deliverables**
> - Updates to **documentation**
> - Updates to **infrastructure**
> - Updates to **coding requirements**
> - Updates to **training**

This section captures the **how** — what concretely will be done.

## Sections at a Glance

| Section | Question Answered |
|---|---|
| Change Description | *What is changing and who asked?* |
| Rationale | *Why are we doing this?* |
| Priority | *How urgent?* |
| Impact | *What does it affect?* |
| Course of Action | *How will we implement it?* |

## Why These Sections Matter

> [!example] Strategic Reasons
> - **Transparency** — everyone sees the change in the same structured form.
> - **Accountability** — initiator and approvers are recorded.
> - **Risk awareness** — the Impact section surfaces unintended consequences.
> - **Alignment** — Course of Action eliminates ambiguity about what gets done.

## When Change Orders Are Triggered

> [!tip] Common Triggers Early in the Lifecycle
> - **Bug identification** during early testing
> - **Customer feedback** from demos or early access
> - **Market requirements** that shifted during development
> - **Discovery findings** that change the assumptions

## Common Pitfalls

> [!warning] What Weakens Change Order Documents
> - **Vague Change Description** — initiator and category unclear.
> - **Missing Rationale** — change approved without understanding why.
> - **No Impact analysis** — knock-on effects discovered too late.
> - **Generic Course of Action** — no concrete steps to execute.

## Summary

A complete change order document includes five sections: **Change Description** (who/what/category), **Rationale** (why/alternatives), **Priority** (urgency), **Impact** (effect on processes and risk), and **Course of Action** (concrete updates to deliverables, docs, infra, code, training). Together these sections enable software teams to manage changes to scope, requirements, and deliverables with **transparency, accountability, and alignment** to project objectives and stakeholder expectations.
