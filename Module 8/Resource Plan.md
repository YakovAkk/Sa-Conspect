# Resource Plan: Team Setup and Allocation

> [!note] Overview
> The ==resource plan== is a critical component of presales. It outlines the **team setup and structure** necessary to deliver the project scope within a defined timeframe. The plan covers roles, responsibilities, and resources — both **storable** (e.g., funds) and **non-storable** (e.g., labor, equipment). It may serve as an **internal document** for commercial calculations or be delivered to the **customer** upon request.

## 1. Team Setup and Structure

> [!info] Core Elements
> - **Roles and Responsibilities** — clearly defined for each team member to ensure efficient execution
> - **Quantity of Resources** — specify the number of team members required for each role

A typical resource plan example illustrates:
- Various project phases
- Associated workstreams
- Roles, **seniority levels**, and headcount per phase
- Start and end dates per position

## 2. Timeline and Phases

> [!info] Time-Based Elements
> - **Start and End Dates** — determine duration per role
> - **Project Phases** — allocate resources to each distinct phase
>
> This ensures **smooth progression** and **timely completion**.

## 3. Considerations for Resource Planning

> [!info] What to Weigh
> - **Seniority Levels** — match expertise with project complexity
> - **Budget Constraints** — optimize cost-effectiveness without compromising quality
> - **Skill Sets** — minimize need for additional training or support

## Resource Plan Elements at a Glance

| Element | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Roles & Responsibilities | Who does what |
| Quantity of Resources | How many of each |
| Start / End Dates | Per-role allocation window |
| Project Phases | Allocation across the project lifecycle |
| Seniority | Match expertise to complexity |
| Budget Constraints | Cost-effective composition |
| Skill Sets | Minimize training overhead |

## 4. Storable vs. Non-Storable Resources

> [!info] Two Categories
> - **Storable** — accumulate over time (e.g., funds, materials)
> - **Non-Storable** — consumed as available (e.g., labor, equipment time)

## 5. Internal vs. Client-Facing

> [!tip] Two Audiences
> - **Internal** — used for commercial calculations, margin analysis, and team allocation
> - **Customer-Facing** — delivered to the client as a transparency artifact
>
> The same resource plan often serves both purposes with **different levels of detail**.

## Why Resource Plans Matter

> [!example] Strategic Reasons
> - **Risk mitigation** — over/under-staffing surfaces before it bites.
> - **Performance optimization** — right people at right time.
> - **Predictable cost** — internal calculations stay honest.
> - **Achievable objectives** — alignment between plan, budget, and timeline.

## Common Pitfalls

> [!warning] What Breaks Resource Plans
> - **Mismatched seniority** — junior team on senior work, or vice versa.
> - **Phase overlap not modeled** — same person counted twice in two phases.
> - **No buffer** — illness, attrition, and ramp-up time ignored.
> - **Skill gaps unacknowledged** — team can't deliver what was sold.

## Summary

A resource plan identifies and allocates **roles, headcount, seniority, dates, and phases** to deliver the project successfully. It balances **budget constraints, skill sets, and timeline phases**, distinguishes between storable and non-storable resources, and may serve as either an internal commercial artifact or a client-facing transparency document. A well-developed plan **mitigates risks, optimizes performance, and achieves project objectives** within defined constraints.
