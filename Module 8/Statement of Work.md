# Statement of Work (SOW)

> [!note] Overview
> A ==Statement of Work (SOW)== is a **formal document** outlining the **work activities and deliverables** a vendor must undertake while providing a service to a client. It is a **customized description** of how a specific project will be executed — covering the client's facility, departments, and personnel involved.

## 1. What the SOW Defines

> [!info] Core SOW Contents
> - **Deliverables** and their schedules
> - **Timelines**
> - **Pricing**
> - **Invoicing schedules**
> - Standard **regulatory and governance** terms
> - **Engagement and pricing models** (e.g., T&M or Fixed-Price)

## 2. MSA vs. SOW

### MSA
The Master Service Agreement is the **overarching document** governing the entire relationship.

> [!info] MSA Characteristics
> - Articulates a **broad corporate stance** on services
> - **One MSA** per company-to-company relationship
> - Sets the foundation

### SOW
The Statement of Work delves into the **specifics of a particular project**.

> [!info] SOW Characteristics
> - **Leverages the MSA** as a foundation
> - **Expands** the MSA to operationalize project delivery
> - **Tailors general terms** to the project's unique circumstances
> - Focuses on **project-specific details** over broad terms
> - **Multiple SOWs** can sit under a single MSA

## MSA vs. SOW at a Glance

| Aspect | MSA | SOW |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | Whole relationship | One project / engagement |
| Quantity per relationship | One | Many possible |
| Content | Broad legal & commercial terms | Specific deliverables, timeline, pricing |
| Reusability | Foundation for many engagements | Specific to one project |
| Audience | Corporate / legal | Project teams |

## 3. Key Sections of an SOW

> [!info] Typical SOW Sections
> - **Terms and termination**
> - **Scope of work**
> - **Service descriptions**
> - **Deliverables and schedules**
> - **Contract types**
> - **Payment schedules**
> - **Responsibilities** of both parties
> - **Acceptance criteria**

## 4. How SOWs Operate

> [!tip] Practical Use
> Once an MSA is in place, each new project uses an SOW to define:
> - Exactly **what** will be delivered
> - **When** each deliverable is due
> - **How much** it costs and how billing works
> - **Who** is responsible for what on each side
> - **How** acceptance is determined

This separation lets the corporate-level terms stay stable while project-level details flex.

## 5. Why the MSA + SOW Pattern Works

> [!example] Strategic Reasons
> - **Stable foundation** — MSA negotiated once, reused many times.
> - **Per-project flexibility** — SOWs adapt to each engagement.
> - **Clear scope** — SOW prevents scope creep with explicit acceptance criteria.
> - **Faster ramp-up** — new projects don't restart legal negotiations.

## Common Pitfalls

> [!warning] What Breaks SOWs
> - **Ambiguous acceptance criteria** — leads to "is this done?" disputes.
> - **Missing payment schedule** — invoicing becomes ad-hoc.
> - **Unclear responsibilities** — work falls between cracks.
> - **No defined termination terms** — exit becomes painful.

## Summary

A Statement of Work is the **project-specific operational contract** that sits under the broader Master Services Agreement. It specifies **deliverables, timelines, pricing, invoicing, responsibilities, and acceptance criteria**. Together, the **MSA + SOW pattern** lets companies maintain a stable legal foundation across many projects while tailoring each project's specifics — accelerating engagement startup and preventing scope conflicts.
