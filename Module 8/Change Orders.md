# Change Orders (CO)

> [!note] Overview
> A ==Change Order (CO)== is a **request to modify the agreed-upon scope of work**, which may or may not adjust the original contract amount or completion date. In software development, changes can affect **business requirements, UI/UX designs, project timelines, or the project's scope and features**. They typically originate from different stakeholders involved in the project.

## 1. What Can Change

> [!info] Common Areas Affected
> - **Business requirements**
> - **UI/UX designs**
> - **Project timelines**
> - **Scope** and features

## 2. Reasons for Initiating a Change Order

### 2.1 Incorrect Estimation
The project scope was **inaccurately estimated**, or obstacles/efficiencies have surfaced requiring deviation from the original plan.

### 2.2 Inefficiency
**Inability to complete required deliverables within budget**, necessitating additional resources.

### 2.3 Additional Services
**Client requests for additional features or options** not initially planned.

## Reasons at a Glance

| Reason | Trigger |
|---|---|
| Incorrect Estimation | Scope mis-sized; obstacles or efficiencies found |
| Inefficiency | Deliverables can't be done in budget |
| Additional Services | Client asks for new features |

## 3. Evaluating a Change Order

> [!warning] Don't Investigate Every Request
> Before launching a detailed analysis, **evaluate the change's value and impact** on:
> - **Timeline**
> - **Pricing**
> - **Scope**
>
> Not all changes warrant extensive investigation. Careful judgment is required.

## 4. Why Change Orders Matter

> [!example] Strategic Reasons
> - **Preserve scope discipline** — every change is explicit and approved.
> - **Protect both parties** — vendor doesn't absorb scope creep silently; client knows what extra costs.
> - **Audit trail** — clear history of why the project deviated.
> - **Realistic timelines** — schedules adjust officially, not informally.

## 5. Change Orders in the Contract Stack

> [!tip] Where COs Fit
> - **MSA** governs the relationship and how changes are handled in general.
> - **SOW** defines the original scope.
> - **Change Orders** formally modify the SOW when scope, timeline, or budget shifts.

## When to Initiate a CO

> [!example] Decision Hints
> - The scope was estimated incorrectly and material rework is needed.
> - The team cannot deliver within budget without more resources.
> - The client wants **new features or options** that weren't originally planned.
> - **Obstacles** (technical or external) force deviation from the plan.
> - **Efficiencies** were discovered that allow better outcomes if the plan changes.

## Common Pitfalls

> [!warning] What Goes Wrong With Change Orders
> - **No process** — changes happen informally and create disputes.
> - **No impact analysis** — agreed without understanding cost/time effect.
> - **Approval bottleneck** — single approver delays everything.
> - **No documentation** — what was changed and why gets lost.

## Summary

Change Orders formally request **modifications to the agreed scope of work**. The three core reasons — **incorrect estimation, inefficiency, and additional services** — cover most cases. Before deep analysis, **evaluate the change's value and impact** on timeline, pricing, and scope. Well-managed change orders **preserve scope discipline, protect both parties, and maintain trust** throughout the engagement.
