# Request for Information (RFI): Process, Format and Architect's Role

> [!note] Overview
> A ==Request for Information (RFI)== is a standard business process used to collect **written details about what different vendors can do**. It follows a clear format so answers can be compared side-by-side. RFIs do **not** usually include detailed solutions or costs — the goal is to share **answers and case studies** demonstrating relevant experience in specific areas and technologies.

## 1. Purpose of an RFI

> [!info] Key Goals
> - Gather standardized information from multiple vendors
> - Compare vendor capabilities side-by-side
> - Pre-qualify candidates before deeper engagement
> - Demonstrate experience through ==case studies== and references

> [!warning] What RFI Is NOT
> RFIs are not the place for detailed solutions or cost breakdowns. Those belong in an RFP.

## 2. Common Formats

RFIs can be shared in several formats depending on client preference:

> [!info] Typical Formats
> - **Excel** — structured tables, easy comparison
> - **Word** — narrative-style answers
> - **Online forms** — web-based intake

## 3. Ownership Inside the Company

> [!tip] Who Collects the Answers
> When a company receives an RFI, the **delivery manager** or **sales manager** is typically responsible for collecting answers. They coordinate with the right specialists to make sure all required information is included.

## 4. The RFI Process

The typical RFI process flows through five stages:

### 4.1 RFI Received
A company receives a Request for Information from a potential client.

### 4.2 Answers Collected
Answers to the questions posed in the RFI are gathered from the relevant teams.

### 4.3 References, Case Studies and Profiles Submitted
Supporting evidence — ==references, case studies, and team profiles== — is provided.

### 4.4 RFI Response Submitted
The response is compiled and submitted following the **prescribed format**.

### 4.5 Demo Conducted
A demo showcasing **operational highlights** of the software is conducted to reinforce the written response.

## RFI Stage Summary

| Stage | What Happens | Owner |
|---|---|---|
| RFI Received | Client request arrives | Sales / Delivery Manager |
| Answers Collected | Internal data gathered | Sales / Delivery Manager + experts |
| References & Case Studies Submitted | Supporting evidence shared | Content team |
| RFI Response Submitted | Final document delivered | Sales / Delivery Manager |
| Demo Conducted | Live walkthrough | Solution Architect + product team |

## 5. Timeline

> [!info] Typical Duration
> The **first three steps** (Received → Answers Collected → References Submitted) usually take about **one week**.

## 6. Architect's Role

> [!tip] Solution Architect Focus
> During the RFI process, Solution Architects are responsible for answering ==different technical questions== — covering capabilities, technology stack, architecture experience, and similar engagements.

## Why RFI Matters

> [!example] Strategic Reasons
> - **First impression** — sets the tone for any future engagement.
> - **Filter step** — only qualifying vendors move on to RFP.
> - **Low-cost discovery** — both sides learn about each other before committing more.
> - **Reusable content** — case studies and profiles often become reusable assets.

## Summary

The RFI is the **initial step** in establishing a working relationship with potential clients. It is a standardized, written exchange where vendors describe their capabilities, share **case studies and references**, and demonstrate experience — without providing detailed solutions or pricing. The process flows through five stages (Received → Answers → References → Submitted → Demo), is typically owned by sales or delivery management, and relies on Solution Architects for technical answers. The first three stages generally take about a week.
